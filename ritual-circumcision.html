The Ceremony & Brit Milah

The ceremony, its meaning, and everything your family needs to know

The Tradition

What is Brit Milah?

Brit milah — literally "the covenant of circumcision" — is one of the most fundamental mitzvot in all of Judaism. Rooted in Genesis 17, when God commanded Abraham to circumcise himself and all male members of his household, brit milah has been observed by the Jewish people continuously for thousands of years, across every era and in every corner of the world. Even in the darkest periods of Jewish history, families risked everything to uphold this covenant. That enduring commitment speaks to just how central brit milah is to Jewish identity.

It is important to understand that a brit milah is not simply a circumcision. A circumcision is a medical procedure. A bris is a covenant between the Jewish people and God that stretches back to Abraham and forward through every generation. When a Jewish boy is brought into the brit, he is welcomed into that ancient, unbroken chain.

The Talmud teaches that milah is equal in weight to all the other mitzvot combined, and that it preceded even the giving of the Torah.

The Ceremony

What to Expect on the Day

The brit milah ceremony is rich with meaning, and every participant has a role to play. The entire ceremony typically takes around 15–20 minutes.

  • ✦
    Kvatter and Kvatterin
    The baby is brought into the room by a kvatter and kvatterin — traditionally a male-female couple, though this is not a strict requirement. Up to six people can be honoured with the privilege of helping carry the baby in, making it a wonderful opportunity to involve multiple family members or close friends. This is considered a great honour and a beautiful way to include loved ones in the ceremony.
  • ✦
    The Chair of Elijah
    Before the procedure begins, the baby is briefly placed on a designated chair known as the Chair of Elijah. The prophet Elijah is traditionally considered a witness and guardian of every brit milah. Inviting his presence connects this ceremony to every brit milah that has ever been performed.
  • ✦
    The Sandek
    The sandek is the person who holds the baby during the circumcision — one of the greatest honours in the ceremony, often compared to offering incense in the Temple. The sandek is typically a grandparent or a deeply respected figure in the family. A second honour, the sandek sheini, is given to someone who holds the baby during the naming blessings that follow — another meaningful way to bring an important person into the heart of the ceremony.
  • ✦
    The Mohel
    The mohel performs the circumcision on behalf of the parents, reciting the blessing and carrying out the procedure with care and precision.
  • ✦
    Blessings and Naming
    Following the circumcision, blessings are recited over wine, and the baby receives his Hebrew name — a deeply moving moment in which the child is formally welcomed into the Jewish people.
Preparation

What to Prepare

Once we have spoken and a date is confirmed, I will be in touch with everything you need to know. In the meantime, here is what to have ready for the day of the brit.

Medical Supplies

  • ✓ Sturdy table — a firm, flat surface at a comfortable working height for the procedure
  • ✓ Infant Tylenol / Acetaminophen (80mg/ml) — to be given after the procedure for comfort during the initial recovery
  • ✓ Petroleum jelly (e.g. Vaseline) — applied to the healing site during diaper changes to prevent sticking

Ritual Items

  • ✓ Kiddush cup — used for the blessings recited over wine during the ceremony
  • ✓ Kosher wine — for the kiddush blessings during the ceremony
  • ✓ Tallit — for men and for women whose practice is to wear a tallit during Jewish ritual

I bring all medical equipment needed for the procedure itself. The items above are what families should have on hand at home. If you have any questions about preparation, don't hesitate to reach out.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Traditionally, brit milah is performed on the eighth day of a boy's life, as commanded in the Torah — counting from the day of birth as day one. Every effort is made to hold the brit on the eighth day. That said, timing can be affected by a variety of factors, so please reach out and we will work out the right timing together.

The Torah teaches that brit milah overrides Shabbat and Yom Kippur when it falls on the eighth day. However, when a brit has been delayed for medical or other reasons, it cannot be performed on Shabbat or Yom Tov. This is determined on a case-by-case basis — please get in touch and we will figure it out together.

The health of the baby always comes first. Brit milah is delayed whenever a baby is unwell or medically unfit for the procedure. This includes prematurity, jaundice requiring treatment, illness, or any other condition flagged by the baby's physician. A delayed brit is performed as soon as the baby has recovered and is medically cleared. Please reach out and we will work together to find the right time.

There are several honoured roles in the brit milah ceremony that provide a meaningful way to involve family and friends. These include the kvatter and kvatterin (who carry the baby in), the sandek (who holds the baby during the circumcision), the sandek sheini (who holds the baby during the naming blessings), and the person designated to recite specific prayers. I am happy to discuss all of these roles with you ahead of the ceremony.

If a baby is born to a mother who is not Jewish, the ceremony is performed as a milah l'shem gerut — a circumcision performed with the intention of conversion, understood as the first step in formally welcoming the child into the Jewish people. The ceremony is very similar, with a small number of changes to the blessings.
