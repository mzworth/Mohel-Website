Overview

What Is Circumcision?

Circumcision is a surgical procedure in which the foreskin — the fold of skin that covers the head of the penis — is removed. It is one of the oldest and most commonly performed surgical procedures in the world, practiced across many cultures and religions for thousands of years. In the Jewish tradition, circumcision holds deep religious significance as a covenant between God and the Jewish people. For other families, the decision may be informed by medical, cultural, or personal considerations.

Families choose circumcision for a variety of reasons. For Jewish families, it is a fundamental religious obligation — the bris milah — performed on the eighth day of a boy's life. For others, the decision is based on established medical benefits, family tradition, or a desire for their son's anatomy to reflect that of his father or community. Whatever your reasons, the most important thing is that the procedure is performed by a qualified, experienced practitioner in a safe and controlled environment.

Medical Benefits

Evidence-Based Benefits of Circumcision

Major medical organizations, including the American Academy of Pediatrics, have concluded that the health benefits of newborn male circumcision outweigh the risks. The procedure has been associated with a number of well-documented medical benefits, particularly when performed in the neonatal period.

  • Reduced risk of urinary tract infections (UTIs): Circumcised males have a significantly lower risk of UTIs in infancy, which can occasionally lead to kidney complications if untreated.
  • Reduced risk of certain sexually transmitted infections: Research suggests circumcision reduces the risk of acquiring certain STIs, including HIV, in heterosexual males.
  • Prevention of phimosis and balanitis: Circumcision eliminates the possibility of phimosis (inability to retract the foreskin) and reduces the risk of balanitis (inflammation of the glans).
  • Reduced risk of penile cancer: While penile cancer is rare, circumcision is associated with a lower lifetime risk.
  • Ease of hygiene: Circumcision simplifies genital hygiene, particularly in early childhood.

It is important for families to make an informed decision in consultation with their physician. The risks of the procedure, while low when performed by a qualified provider, should also be understood and discussed.

Safety

Safety of the Procedure

Neonatal circumcision is one of the safest surgical procedures performed when carried out by a trained and experienced practitioner under appropriate conditions. Complications are uncommon and are most often minor — such as minor bleeding or localized infection — and are readily managed. Serious complications are rare.

What makes circumcision safe is the combination of technical expertise, proper pain management, sterile technique, and attentive aftercare guidance. As a Royal College certified emergency physician with formal training specifically in neonatal circumcision, and with that expertise recognized by the College of Physicians and Surgeons of Ontario, I bring a level of medical preparation to this procedure that goes well beyond what most practitioners can offer. I use appropriate anaesthetic technique, maintain a sterile field throughout, and provide thorough aftercare instructions to every family.

Technique

The Mogen Clamp Technique

[Image of Mogen clamp to be inserted here]
Alt text: Mogen clamp used in neonatal circumcision by Toronto mohel

The Mogen clamp is the instrument of choice for ritual circumcision among trained mohalim and is also widely used by physicians performing neonatal circumcision. It is a simple, elegant instrument with an excellent safety record when used by an experienced practitioner.

The clamp works by protecting the glans while a precisely controlled amount of foreskin is clamped and excised. Because the Mogen clamp is applied in a single motion and the procedure is completed very quickly — typically in under a minute — it minimizes the duration of discomfort for the infant. The speed and precision of the technique, combined with appropriate pain management, contributes to its excellent safety profile.

The Mogen clamp has been used in Jewish ritual circumcision for generations and remains the standard instrument in the hands of certified mohalim trained through programs such as BRIT KODESH. Its combination of traditional use and strong medical outcomes makes it the ideal choice for a bris milah performed by a physician-mohel.

On the Day

What to Expect on the Day of the Procedure

Prior to the procedure, I will review the completed intake form with the family and confirm that the infant is in good health and appropriate for the procedure. I will explain each step to the parents and answer any remaining questions before we begin.

The infant will be placed comfortably and securely. A topical and/or injected local anaesthetic is used to minimize pain — I take pain management seriously and do not perform the procedure without appropriate anaesthesia. The procedure itself, using the Mogen clamp technique, is completed very quickly. Parents are typically present throughout.

Following the procedure, I will apply a dressing and provide detailed written aftercare instructions. I am available to answer questions in the days following the bris. Most infants recover quickly and are feeding normally within a short time after the procedure. The healing process typically completes within one to two weeks.
