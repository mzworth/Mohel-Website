The Procedure

My approach to safe, careful, and compassionate circumcision in Toronto and the GTA.

Overview

About Circumcision

Circumcision is a surgical procedure in which the foreskin is removed from the penis. It is one of the oldest and most widely performed surgical procedures in the world, practiced across many cultures and religions for thousands of years. For Jewish families, circumcision is performed as brit milah — a covenant of deep religious and cultural significance — rather than primarily for medical reasons.

Some medical organizations, including the American Academy of Pediatrics, have noted that research suggests there may be potential medical benefits associated with circumcision, including a possible reduction in the risk of certain urinary tract infections and sexually transmitted infections. However, the evidence is not conclusive enough to recommend circumcision as a routine medical procedure, and the AAP notes that the health benefits are not sufficient to recommend universal newborn circumcision. For families considering brit milah, the decision is ultimately a personal one rooted in Jewish tradition and identity rather than medical indication. You can read the AAP's 2012 Policy Statement on Circumcision for a thorough review of the evidence.

My Technique

How I Perform the Procedure

As both a Royal College emergency physician and a certified mohel, I bring a level of clinical preparation to every brit milah that I believe makes a meaningful difference. Below is an overview of my approach.

  • ✦
    The Mogen Clamp
    I use the Mogen clamp, a traditional instrument favoured by many mohalim for its excellent safety profile. The Mogen clamp minimizes bleeding and allows the procedure to be completed extremely quickly — typically in under a minute — which minimizes the duration of discomfort for the infant. Its combination of speed, precision, and long-standing use in The Ceremony makes it my instrument of choice.
  • ✦
    Nerve Block for Pain Management
    Prior to the procedure, I administer a dorsal penile nerve block — a local anaesthetic injection — so that the infant does not feel pain during the circumcision. I take pain management seriously and do not proceed without appropriate anaesthesia in place. This is both medically standard and consistent with the halachic principle of minimizing suffering.
  • ✦
    Sucrose for Comfort
    I also use oral sucrose — a small amount of sugar water — administered just before and during the procedure. Research has shown that sucrose has a natural calming and analgesic effect in newborns, helping to keep the infant settled throughout. It is a simple and well-evidenced comfort measure that I incorporate as a routine part of my approach.
  • ✦
    Aftercare
    Following the procedure I apply a dressing and provide every family with detailed written aftercare instructions. I am available to answer questions in the days following the bris. Most infants recover quickly and healing typically completes within one to two weeks.
Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

As with any surgical procedure, circumcision carries some risks. The most common are minor bleeding and localized infection at the site. However, serious bleeding or infection are extremely rare when the procedure is performed by a trained and experienced practitioner using proper sterile technique. I will discuss the risks with you before the procedure so you can make a fully informed decision.

I administer a dorsal penile nerve block — a local anaesthetic injection — prior to the procedure so that the infant does not feel pain during the circumcision. I also use oral sucrose to help keep the infant calm and comfortable. I do not proceed without appropriate anaesthesia in place, and I will walk you through my pain management approach before we begin.

The risks of neonatal circumcision are low when performed by a qualified practitioner. The most common complications are minor bleeding or infection, both of which are readily managed. Serious complications are rare. As a Royal College emergency physician I am well-equipped to recognize and respond to any complications should they arise. I will review the risks with you in full before proceeding.

The Mogen clamp is the instrument of choice for many mohalim and has an excellent safety record in experienced hands. It minimizes bleeding, allows the procedure to be completed very quickly — typically under a minute — and has a long history of use in The Ceremony. Its speed and precision are key advantages for minimizing the infant's discomfort.

Sucrose is simply sugar water, administered orally in a small amount just before and during the procedure. Research has demonstrated that sucrose has a natural calming and mild analgesic effect in newborns. It is a safe, simple, and well-evidenced comfort measure that I use as a routine part of my approach alongside the nerve block.

Most infants heal fully within one to two weeks. The site may appear red or have a small amount of yellowish discharge in the first few days — this is normal. I provide detailed written aftercare instructions to every family and am available for questions during the healing period.

Aftercare is straightforward. You will receive written instructions covering how to keep the area clean, how to apply petroleum jelly to prevent the healing skin from sticking to the diaper, and what signs to watch for. I am available to answer any questions that come up during the healing process.

In Jewish tradition, brit milah is performed on the eighth day of the boy's life — which also aligns well with medical recommendations for the optimal timing of neonatal circumcision. If there are any health concerns, the bris may be delayed until the infant is ready. We will discuss timing during our initial conversation.
